1. Step Functions:

Advantageous situation:

When you suspect the relationship between features and the target variable might be non-linear and potentially vary locally across different data regions.

Example:

Imagine you're modeling stock prices and suspect the price might not follow a simple linear trend over time. Local regression can capture these local trends and potentially adapt to changing relationships over time.

2. Local Regression:

Advantageous situation:

When you suspect the relationship between features and the target variable might be non-linear and potentially vary locally across different data regions.

Example:

Imagine you're modeling stock prices and suspect the price might not follow a simple linear trend over time. Local regression can capture these local trends and potentially adapt to changing relationships over time.

3. GAMs (Generalized Additive Models):

Advantageous situation:

When you have a mix of linear and non-linear relationships between features and the target variable, and want more flexibility in modeling these relationships.

Example:

Imagine you're modeling customer churn based on factors like purchase history (numerical) and satisfaction level (categorical). GAMs can handle the numerical purchase history linearly while potentially capturing a non-linear relationship with the categorical satisfaction level.

Question 3

Define the following types of smooth functions from PYGAM library (https://pygam.readthedocs.io/en/latest/notebooks/tour_of_pygam.html ) and list at least one relevant potential use:

a) l() linear terms

b) s() spline terms

c) f() factor terms

d) te() Tensor product interaction terms

a) l() linear terms

Definition:

The l() function represents linear terms in a Generalized Additive Model (GAM). It models a linear relationship between a continuous feature (numerical variable) and the target variable.

Use Case:

When you have features with a proportional relationship to the target variable. For example, modeling house prices as a function of square footage (assuming price increases linearly with increasing square footage).

b) s() spline terms

Definition:

The s() function represents smooth spline terms in a GAM. It allows for modeling non-linear relationships between a feature and the target variable. Splines are piecewise polynomial functions that provide flexibility in capturing smooth curves.

Use Case:

When you suspect a non-linear trend in the data, like modeling sales figures over time (which may exhibit seasonal patterns) or customer churn probability based on a customer's age (which might not be a simple linear increase).

c) f() factor terms

Definition: The f() function represents categorical features (also known as factor variables) in a GAM. It encodes the categories into a set of dummy variables, allowing the model to capture different effects for each category on the target variable.

Use Case:

When you have categorical predictors that might influence the target variable in different ways. For example, modeling customer satisfaction based on customer type (bronze, silver, gold) where each category might have a distinct satisfaction level.

d) te() Tensor product interaction terms

Definition: The te() function represents tensor product interaction terms in a GAM. It allows modeling the interaction effect between two or more features. The interaction term captures how the effect of one feature on the target variable changes depending on the level of another feature.

Use Case:

When you suspect that the effect of one feature on the target variable depends on the level of another feature. For example, modeling website click-through rate (CTR) considering both user demographics (age) and product category. The CTR for a particular product category might vary depending on the user's age group. By combining these smooth functions in a GAM, you can build flexible and powerful models that capture complex relationships between features and the target variable.

Question 4

Briefly explain what spatial and temporal autocorrelation are? List one way in which GAMs could help to account for this (e.g. indicate the type of smooth function you would use or potential types of correlation structures).

Spatial Autocorrelation:

Spatial autocorrelation describes the clustering or patterning of a variable across geographic locations. It measures the degree to which nearby observations are more similar (positive autocorrelation) or dissimilar (negative autocorrelation) compared to random chance.

Example: Disease outbreaks in a city might exhibit positive spatial autocorrelation, indicating higher infection rates in neighboring areas.

Temporal Autocorrelation:

Temporal autocorrelation describes the correlation of a variable over time. It measures the degree to which observations at close time points are more similar (positive autocorrelation) or dissimilar (negative autocorrelation) compared to random chance.

Example: Stock prices often exhibit positive temporal autocorrelation, meaning past price movements can influence future movements.

Using GAMs to Account for Autocorrelation

GAMs (Generalized Additive Models) can be a powerful tool to account for autocorrelation issues in both spatial and temporal data analysis. Here's how:

1. Smoothing with Spline Terms (s())

Spline terms in GAMs allow for modeling non-linear trends across space or time. This flexibility can capture underlying spatial or temporal patterns that might contribute to autocorrelation. For example, a spline term for location coordinates in a disease outbreak model could capture spatial clustering, while a spline term for time in a stock price model could capture temporal trends and seasonality.

2. Interaction Terms (te())

Tensor product interaction terms (te()) can incorporate spatial or temporal coordinates as interacting features. This allows the model to capture how the effect of other variables on the target variable changes depending on location or time. For example, you could create an interaction term between a spatial coordinate and a pollution source variable to model how pollution levels vary depending on location relative to the source. Similarly, you could interact a time variable with a weather variable to account for how weather seasonality affects sales.

$$ \hat{g}1 = \arg\min_{g}(\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - g(x_i))^2 + \lambda \int [g^{(3)} (x)]^2 dx ) $$

$$ \hat{g}2 = \arg\min_{g}(\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - g(x_i))^2 + \lambda \int [g^{(4)} (x)]^2 dx ) $$

where $g^{(m)}$ represents the $m^{th}$ derivative of $g$. Now, answer the following questions (and include a brief explanation):

a) As $\lambda \rightarrow \infty$, will $\hat{g}1$ or $\hat{g}2$ have smaller training RSS?

  • For both $\hat{g}_1$ and $\hat{g}_2$, the penalty term $\lambda \int [g^{(m)}(x)]^2 dx$ dominates the objective function.
  • Since the penalty term penalizes higher-order derivatives of $g$, as $\lambda \rightarrow \infty$, both $\hat{g}_1$ and $\hat{g}_2$ will tend towards smoother functions with smaller derivatives.
  • However, since $\hat{g}_2$ has a penalty term on the fourth derivative ($g^{(4)}$) compared to the penalty term on the third derivative ($g^{(3)}$) for $\hat{g}_1$, $\hat{g}_2$ will tend to be smoother than $\hat{g}_1$.
  • Therefore, as $\lambda \rightarrow \infty$, $\hat{g}_2$ will have a smaller training Residual Sum of Squares (RSS) compared to $\hat{g}_1$ because it is penalized more for higher-order variations.

b) As $\lambda \rightarrow \infty$, will $\hat{g}1$ or $\hat{g}2$ have smaller test RSS?

  • As $\lambda \rightarrow \infty$, both $\hat{g}_1$ and $\hat{g}_2$ will become overly smooth and may not capture the true underlying relationship between the predictors and the response variable well.
  • Consequently, both $\hat{g}_1$ and $\hat{g}_2$ are likely to have larger test RSS compared to when $\lambda$ is appropriately chosen (i.e., not too large).
  • However, $\hat{g}_2$, being smoother due to the penalty on the fourth derivative, may have a slightly smaller test RSS compared to $\hat{g}_1$ in some cases, but the difference might not be substantial.

c) For $\lambda= 0$, will $\hat{g}1$ or $\hat{g}2$ have smaller training RSS?

  • When $\lambda = 0$, the penalty term becomes negligible, and the objective functions reduce to the ordinary least squares (OLS) regression.
  • Both $\hat{g}_1$ and $\hat{g}_2$ will be determined solely by the sum of squares of residuals without any penalty for the smoothness of the functions.
  • Since $\hat{g}_2$ has a penalty term on the fourth derivative, it might try to fit the data more closely, potentially resulting in a smaller training RSS compared to $\hat{g}_1$.
  • Therefore, for $\lambda = 0$, $\hat{g}_2$ is likely to have a smaller training RSS compared to $\hat{g}_1$.
